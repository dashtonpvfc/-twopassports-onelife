We woke at around 9am, refreshed and ready for another day on the North Coast 500. Today's destination was John O'Groats, but as with most days on this trip, the journey itself would prove to be just as memorable as the destination. The route north offered plenty of opportunities to stop and explore, and we were in no hurry to rush through such spectacular scenery.

Our first stop was the magnificent Dunrobin Castle. Standing proudly on the coast overlooking the Moray Firth, it looked more like a French château than a Scottish castle. As the most northerly of Scotland's great houses, Dunrobin has an incredible history stretching back to the early 1300s and boasts an impressive 189 rooms. Although we decided to save a few pounds and admire it from the outside rather than paying to tour the interior, we still spent time wandering around the beautiful grounds and taking in the grandeur of the place. Even from a distance, it was easy to appreciate why it is one of Scotland's most iconic stately homes.

Continuing north, we stopped in the charming fishing village of Helmsdale for a coffee at the River Café. Situated beside the river, it was a lovely spot to sit and watch the world go by. While we enjoyed our drinks, Mel picked up a book for Theo called My Granny Went to Scotland, a fitting souvenir from our Highland adventure.

As we pushed further north, the scenery became noticeably wilder and more dramatic. The roads felt more remote, the landscapes more rugged, and sheep seemed to have little regard for the rules of the road, casually wandering across our path whenever they pleased. It was exactly the sort of driving experience we'd hoped for when planning the NC500.

One of the true highlights of the day came when we reached Duncansby Head. We parked up and walked towards the lighthouse before continuing to the Geo of Sclaites and the famous Duncansby Stacks. The views were simply breathtaking. Towering sea stacks rose dramatically from the water below while waves crashed against the cliffs. Armed with binoculars, we spent a couple of hours wildlife spotting and were delighted to see a huge variety of seabirds, including one of the stars of the Scottish coastline — a puffin. Watching these charismatic birds perched on the cliffs was a genuine bucket-list moment. The combination of dramatic geology, abundant wildlife and endless sea views made this one of the most awe-inspiring places of the entire trip.

By around 3:30pm, we arrived at John O'Groats Campsite and settled into a lovely hardstanding pitch. With the motorhome set up, it was time to relax. We sat beneath the awning with a cold beer while Mel put together a simple but perfect lunch of ham and cheese wraps with crisps. Sometimes it's the uncomplicated moments during a motorhome trip that are the most enjoyable.

![Settling into John O'Groats Campsite](20260525_154038.jpg)
![Relaxing under the awning](20260525_155357.jpg)

Later in the afternoon, we wandered into the small settlement of John O'Groats itself. While much of the rest of the UK was sweltering in a heatwave, the far north of Scotland remained pleasantly cool at around 17 degrees with plenty of cloud cover drifting overhead. The weather felt ideal for exploring.

![Wandering into John O'Groats](20260525_164828.jpg)

Naturally, we had to do the full tourist experience and queue up for photographs beside the famous John O'Groats signpost. Cameras clicked from every direction as visitors celebrated reaching one of Britain's most iconic locations.

The afternoon then took a very enjoyable turn when we discovered John O'Groats Brewery and The Last Pub. Both were packed with character and full of friendly locals and fellow travellers. We sampled several excellent local beers, including Red MacGregor Ruby Ale and the aptly named Deep Groat, brewed just next door. The atmosphere was fantastic, with no shortage of colourful characters and interesting conversations.

Back at the motorhome, the evening unfolded perfectly. A couple more beers appeared, including an Old Crafty Hen and an Adnams Broadside, as we settled into our chairs and watched the sun gradually sink towards the horizon. The view across the sea was absolutely stunning. The colours reflected across the water and the vast open sky seemed to stretch forever. It was one of those moments that makes travelling in a motorhome so special — good company, a cold drink in hand and a sunset that simply stopped us in our tracks.
